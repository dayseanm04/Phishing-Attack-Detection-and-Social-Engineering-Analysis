# Security Recommendations

## Overview

Based on the analysis of 10 email examples in this lab, the following recommendations are provided to help individuals and organizations better identify, avoid, and respond to phishing attacks. These lessons are drawn directly from the patterns and tactics observed across the suspicious emails reviewed.

---

## 1. Always Verify the Sender's Email Address

The most consistent red flag across all phishing examples was a mismatched or suspicious sender domain. Before opening attachments or clicking any links, always check the full email address — not just the display name.

- Government agencies will always use a **.gov** domain (e.g., @fdic.gov)
- Legitimate companies will use their official corporate domain (e.g., @norton.com, @dropbox.com)
- Free email providers like Gmail, Yahoo, or foreign domains being used to represent official organizations is an immediate red flag
- If the display name says one thing but the email address says another, treat the email as suspicious

---

## 2. Do Not Click Links or Download Attachments From Unverified Senders

Several examples in this lab used malicious links and attachments as their primary attack vector.

- Hover over links before clicking to preview the actual destination URL
- Be especially cautious of links using **HTTP** instead of HTTPS — unencrypted links are unsafe
- Treat unexpected attachments especially **.pdf**, **.html**, or executable files as potentially dangerous
- When in doubt, navigate directly to the organization's official website rather than clicking the provided link

---

## 3. Be Skeptical of Urgent or Fear-Based Language

Urgency is one of the most commonly used tactics in phishing emails. Scammers manufacture panic to bypass critical thinking.

- Phrases like "your account has been suspended," "immediate action required," or "you will be charged" are designed to make you react without thinking
- Take a moment to pause and evaluate before responding to any email that pressures you to act quickly
- Legitimate organizations give customers reasonable time to respond and provide multiple ways to verify the situation

---

## 4. Never Send Personal Information via Email

Multiple examples in this lab requested personal details such as full name, address, phone number, email, and country directly through email.

- No legitimate bank, government agency, or company will ask you to reply to an email with sensitive personal information
- Never send financial details, passwords, Social Security numbers, or identification documents via email
- If an organization genuinely needs your information, log in directly to your account through their official website

---

## 5. Verify Unexpected Financial Notifications Independently

The fake Norton renewal and fake FDIC emails both used financial urgency to manipulate recipients. If you receive an unexpected charge or billing notification:

- Do not call the number provided in the email
- Look up the company's official customer service number directly from their website
- Log in to your account directly to verify whether any charges or changes have actually been made

---

## 6. Recognize and Ignore Unrealistic Offers

Advance-fee scams like the Military Inheritance Scam and the Fake Bank Draft rely on the appeal of large, unexpected financial rewards.

- No stranger will send you $500,000 or a $1.5 million bank draft through an unsolicited email
- If an offer sounds too good to be true, it is
- These scams eventually ask victims to pay upfront "fees," "taxes," or "processing costs" before any money is delivered — money that is never returned
- Delete and report these emails immediately and do not engage with the sender

---

## 7. Pay Attention to Grammar, Spelling, and Formatting

Poor writing quality is a reliable signal that an email is not from a professional organization.

- Look for misspelled words, missing spaces, inconsistent capitalization, and awkward sentence structure
- Be cautious of emails that use bold text aggressively to highlight alarming phrases, this is a manipulation tactic
- Legitimate organizations proofread their communications and maintain consistent, professional formatting
